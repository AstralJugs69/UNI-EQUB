Abstract
In Ethiopia, Equb (Rotating Savings and Credit Association) serves as a vital traditional financial instrument for saving and social bonding. However, the traditional manual execution of Equb is often plagued by challenges such as lack of transparency, geographical limitations, mismanagement of funds, and disputes regarding the lottery process. These issues are particularly pronounced in a dynamic university environment where students and staff have tight schedules and limited physical interaction time for such financial gatherings.
The primary objective of this project, Uni-equb, is to design and develop a web-based management system that digitizes the traditional Equb process tailored specifically for the university community. The system aims to automate the core functionalities of Equb, including group creation, member registration, contribution tracking, and the lottery selection process.
To achieve this, the project utilized a structured system development methodology. Requirements were gathered through interviews and questionnaires distributed among university students and staff to understand the specific needs and pain points of the target users. Object-Oriented Analysis and Design (OOAD) techniques, including Use Case and Sequence diagrams, were employed to model the system's architecture. The application was developed using modern web technologies to ensure responsiveness and accessibility.
The resulting system successfully provides a secure and transparent platform where users can join Equb groups, pay contributions digitally, and view real-time status updates. The automated lottery algorithm ensures fairness, eliminating human bias. Consequently, Uni-equb not only modernizes a cultural financial practice but also enhances financial inclusion and trust within the university community.

CHAPTER ONE: INTRODUCTION
Background of the Study
The management of personal finances presents a significant challenge for university students worldwide. Often operating with limited budgets from part-time work, family support, or student loans, students must balance essential living expenses, educational materials, and social activities. This financial strain is particularly acute in developing economies, where economic instability and inflation can rapidly erode purchasing power.
The traditional Equb system has long served as an effective community-based financial practice that encourages saving, trust, and mutual support. Equb is a rotating savings and credit association in which members contribute a fixed amount of money within a specified period, and the total sum is awarded to one member in each cycle. This system promotes cooperation, discipline, and a sense of financial responsibility among participants. Despite its long-standing success and cultural importance, the traditional Equb method is primarily manual and face-to-face, which limits its convenience and efficiency, especially in modern urban and academic settings.
Furthermore, the proposed system aligns with Ethiopia’s ongoing efforts toward digital transformation and financial inclusion. By combining cultural traditions with modern technological solutions, Uni- Equb not only preserves the essence of the Equb practice but also adapts it to meet contemporary financial and social needs. Ultimately, this initiative contributes to the empowerment of students, enabling them to manage their finances more effectively and to develop responsible saving habits that will benefit them beyond their academic years.
Statement of the Problem
University students often face financial difficulties due to limited and inconsistent sources of income, inadequate budgeting skills, and a lack of structured saving mechanisms. These financial challenges can negatively affect their academic performance, and overall quality of life. University students face a persistent and multifaceted problem of financial.insecurity and a lack of accessible savings mechanisms. The existing financial infrastructure often fails to meet their specific needs, resulting in several critical issues:
Inaccessibility of Formal Banking: Many students find formal savings accounts cumbersome due to excessive service charges, low interest returns, and bureaucratic hurdles. Loan facilities are typically inaccessible due to a lack of credit history or collateral.
Lack of Financial Discipline: Without a structured and motivating system, students struggle to cultivate consistent saving habits. Their limited income is often consumed by immediate expenses, leaving little to no room for future planning, emergency funds, or investment in their educational needs.
Inefficiency of Informal Saving: While traditional, physical Equbs are popular, they are not without drawbacks. They are logistically challenging to organize, vulnerable to mismanagement or default, and require physical presence for collection and distribution, which is impractical for a dispersed student body.
Digital Divide in Finance: There is a significant gap in the market for financial technology (FinTech) solutions that are tailored to the socio-economic context and cultural norms of Ethiopian students.
Therefore, the central problem is the absence of a secure, convenient, and culturally resonant financial tool that empowers university students to save money consistently, access lump-sum funds when needed, and build a foundation for long-term financial literacy and stability
.

To develop a mobile-based UniEqub application for university students.

To accomplish the general objective of the project, the following specific objectives are defined as sequential subtasks:

Scope of the Study
The scope of this study encompasses designing, developing, and evaluating a mobile-based application known as UniEqub, specifically developed for university students to facilitate digital savings and group contribution management within the university environment. This study covers both the technical aspects of the system implementation and how the system is put into practice in enhancing traditional equb operations among students.
The proposed system will be developed as a mobile application that will target the Android mobile platform. This is because most students in the campus environment are using Android smartphones. The UniEqub mobile app allows the users to create, manage, and participate in equb groups digitally.
This study will be geographically and functionally limited to one university environment where the test and evaluation of the application would be conducted. While this current scope of work focuses on the university level, the design and architecture shall be flexible in a way that would allow its future scalability, such as adding cross-campus functionality, real payment integrations, or web-based support.

Significance of the project
Uni-Equb is far more than a savings application; it serves as a transformative tool that was designed to meet the unique financial challenges that university students face. By digitizing the conventional "Equb" system, it offers value across many dimensions to its unique user groupings:
 
Uni-Equb is a fundamental tool for financial empowerment and crisis management, from which students can begin to manage their finances formally and intuitively. It helps students build steady savings habits and financial discipline since they operate within something very familiar. Thus, it directly addresses the most pressing needs of students through offering a reliable mechanism to liquidate a sum of money during emergencies, such as a broken laptop or medical costs, or for major purchases like textbooks, without recourse to high-interest loans. Such smoothing of cash flow reduces a considerable source of stress, enabling students to focus more effectively on their academic pursuits. 
For student organizers, Uni-Equb acts as a powerful management tool that simplifies how Equb groups operate. It automates the entire process — from member registration to contribution tracking — and reduces the burden of manual coordination. Organizers no longer have to worry about cash handling, recording contributions, or following up on delayed payments. The system automatically keeps transparent records, sends reminders, and determines whose turn it is to receive the pooled fund. This automation helps organizers save time, maintain fairness, and focus more on group engagement and growth rather than dealing with repetitive administrative tasks.
For platform administrators, UniEqub provides a robust, data-driven framework designed to manage complex peer-to-peer financial cycles with minimal manual overhead. The system centralizes administrative oversight through automated transaction logs, real-time status monitoring, and system-wide audit trails. By automating the lottery logic and payment verification, the platform reduces the risk of human error and administrative fatigue.
 
At the systemic and community levels, Uni-Equb serves as a catalyst for digital inclusion and social cohesion. The project modernizes a trusted cultural tradition, connects heritage with innovation, and opens routes to a cashless campus ecosystem. It reinforces the community bonds through building digital trust networks with peers and promotes a culture of mutual support and collective responsibility. Furthermore, the project positions itself as a scalable homegrown FinTech solution contributing to the local digital economy and showing how technology can be used to solve particular community challenges, thus empowering a new generation to engage with financial technology with confidence.

Agile Development: The Agile approach is chosen for its flexibility and iterative nature. As a mobile app project with a small team, this allows us to adapt to changing requirements and get user feedback (from our team and test users) in short cycles.
Object-Oriented (OO) Design: The OO approach is selected for analysis and design, as it focuses on combining data and processes into objects. This is ideal for a system like Uni- Equb, which will manage real-world entities such as 'Users', 'Equb Groups', and 'Contributions'. This approach will allow us to build a modular, scalable, and maintainable system using UML diagrams to model the system before implementation.

System Implementation
The implementation (coding) phase will follow an Object-Oriented Programming (OOP) paradigm. This approach is based on the concept of "objects" which may contain both data (attributes) and code (methods).
By using React Native (which is component-based and naturally aligns with OO principles) and Supabase, the application will be designed as a collection of interacting objects (e.g., User, EqubGroup, Payment, Notification) that model the real-world domain. This method is chosen over procedural programming as it directly supports the Object-Oriented design from the previous section, leading to more modular, reusable, and maintainable code.
Testing and Deployment Methodology
The testing and deployment methodology is designed to ensure the quality, reliability, and usability of the Uni-Equb application.
Testing Methodology Our testing strategy will investigate and provide information about the quality of the software by incorporating recognized levels of tests:
Unit Testing: Individual components and functions will be tested in isolation.
Integration Testing: Tested units will be combined and tested to ensure they
interact correctly.
Component Interface Testing: This will focus on verifying the "glue" between different parts of the app, such as navigation between screens.
System Testing: The entire application will be tested from an end-user perspective to verify it meets all functional requirements.
Specialized Testing:

(B) If the sandbox is inaccessible, a mocked local USSD pipeline will be developed to simulate the payment workflow. A mocked testing dataset of users, groups, and transactions will be created to ensure the system can be tested thoroughly.
Deployment Methodology The deployment process will follow a phased approach:

Internal Testing (Alpha): An .apk (Android Package) file will be generated and distributed to the four team members for installation and testing on physical devices.
User Acceptance Testing (Beta): After internal testing, the .apk will be shared with a small, select group of target users.
Final Submission: The stable .apk file will be submitted as the final project deliverable.
Security Methodology
Security stays very important for UniEqub because user financial information is involved. By using these approaches below, protection, integrity, and privacy of data are all ensured:
Authentication and authorization: Each user makes a personalized account that gets checked with their university email or ID. Process includes:
Secure Login System: User passwords go through new adaptive hashing methods such as bcrypt or Argon2 for safety.
Two-Factor Authentication (2FA): An OTP, meaning a One-Time Password, will be emailed or texted to the person for main actions like withdrawing or starting any group.
Role-Based Access Control (RBAC): Every user has special permissions, for example admin for a group or just a member.
Database Security The database will be set up as follows:
Admins with restricted access (project admins only change database schema).
Routine patching and updates are done for the database.
Foreign key constraint applies to verify data integrity and helps avoid unauthorized alter.
Secure Communication
HTTPS Protocol: The whole communication between app and server gets carried out in HTTPS, so interception is prevented.
Token-Based Authentication: Tokens are used to secure user session, minimizing chance of hijack.
Backup and Recovery Methodology
Objective: To ensure that all user data, app settings, and financial records in Uniequb are securely backed up and can be quickly restored in case of accidental deletion, hardware failure, or system errors.
Backup Strategy:
Automated Cloud Backups: All critical data (user profiles, transactions, and app settings) will be automatically backed up to the cloud daily. The cloud provider (e.g., Supabase or Firebase) offers versioned backups to allow restoration to previous states.
Local Device Caching (Optional): Recent activity and unsynced transactions are temporarily stored on the user’s device in encrypted form, allowing recovery if network issues occur before cloud sync.
Redundancy: Backups are stored in multiple regions (where supported by the cloud provider) to prevent data loss from localized outages.
Recovery Process:
User-Initiated Recovery: Users can restore their account data from the latest cloud
backup through the app’s settings if they switch devices or reinstall the app.
System Recovery: In case of server failure, the system administrator can restore the database from the most recent cloud backup. The recovery process is tested quarterly to ensure minimal downtime and integrity of restored data.
Security Measures:
All backup data is encrypted in transit and at rest using industry-standard encryption   
(e.g., AES-256).Access to backup systems is restricted to authorized personnel only.
Regular audits are conducted to ensure backup integrity and compliance with data protection standards.
Frequency:
Daily automatic backups for all critical user and app data.
Weekly full backups for system configurations and less frequently changed data.
Quarterly disaster recovery tests to validate restoration procedures.

CHAPTER TWO: DESCRIPTIONS OF EXISTING SYSTEM
2.1 Introduction of the Existing System
In the current environment, students at the university who wish to save or participate in group-based financial activities such as Equb have to rely on traditional, manual methods. An Equb is a source of informal savings association that helps individuals develop financial discipline and access much-needed pooled resources. Despite being culturally important, the Equb system that students currently use has not been modernized and remains highly paper-based and informal.
Students typically form Equb groups with their classmates, roommates, or close friends. One person typically serves as the collector and record keeper for the management process, which is handled manually. A handwritten note, verbal confirmation, or messaging apps like Telegram and WhatsApp are typically used for contribution tracking. Although these techniques are somewhat successful, they are not sustainable approaches to money management. Missed contributions, inaccurate recording, and payment delays are the outcomes of an unstructured or automated system.
Lack of accountability and transparency are two more significant issues with this system. Participants must rely on informal agreements and trust because there is no central location for data storage. It becomes challenging to resolve misunderstandings or conflicts that occur, such as when a member questions the accuracy of records or someone forgets to pay. The lack of digital records that can be verified raises the risk of disagreements and may even cause the Equb group as a whole to fall apart. Students, who might not have a lot of experience with financial systems, are particularly prone to this issue.
Another shortcoming of the present system is that it is geographically bound. In order for a joint savings group to be formed, students need to be practically in close vicinity with one another. Many students who are living in different dorms cannot easily start or be part of an Equb group. This excludes a lot of students who could otherwise benefit from saving collectively since the process is heavily dependent on physical meetings and personal interaction.
Furthermore, there is no technological integration in the current system. Despite the fact that almost all students own mobile devices, no specialized digital platform has been created to effectively oversee the Equb activities. Students are not yet aware of the main advantages of digital solutions, such as transaction history tracking, digital wallet security, automated payables reminders, and performance analytics. Relying too much on these more manual approaches not only reduces convenience but also denies students the skills they need to manage their finances in a digital age.
Some students might also resist this change organizationally due to comfort with acquired habits or fear of adapting to new technology. Others may view digital financial systems as a threat to privacy or distrust them. For this reason, even when students realize the weaknesses of the manual approach, most are very reluctant to explore innovative solutions. However, such resistance contributes to the perpetuation of inefficiency and lowers the potential impact that could be achieved with savings initiatives among students.
All things considered, university students manage Equb savings through a manual, unstructured, and ineffective system. The current system is very vulnerable to human error and potential conflict because it depends on verbal communication, a high degree of trust, and manual record-keeping. Transparency, automation of contributions, and participation across campuses are not provided by any digital mechanism. Furthermore, students frequently don't know about alternative strategies or technological advancements that could enhance their experience saving money.
 
2.2 Strength of Existing System
The existing system of Equb, especially in a university or student community context, has several notable strengths that contribute to its continuous practice and popularity. Although the traditional Equb operates manually without digital tools, it still serves as an effective and reliable informal financial system. This section discusses the major strengths of the current Equb system as observed among university students and community groups.
 
1. Simplicity and Ease of Understanding
One of the strongest aspects of the existing Equb system is its simplicity. The concept and operation are easy to understand, even for individuals who have limited knowledge of finance or technology. Each member contributes a fixed amount of money at regular intervals, and the collected sum is given to one participant in each round. This simple cycle continues until every member has received their share. Because the process is straightforward, members can easily participate without needing special training or complicated recordkeeping. This simplicity makes it accessible to all types of users, including students who may not have prior experience with formal financial systems.
 
2. Social Trust and Cooperation
Another major strength of the existing Equb system is the high level of social trust and cooperation among its members. Typically, Equb groups are formed among friends, classmates, or individuals who share a strong social bond. This personal connection promotes honesty, transparency, and accountability. Members rely on mutual trust rather than legal contracts, which reduces conflict and increases cooperation. In a university setting, this social trust helps students support one another financially, especially in times of need such as covering tuition fees, purchasing study materials, or managing living expenses.
 
3. Financial Inclusion and Accessibility
The traditional Equb system provides financial access to individuals who may not qualify for formal banking services. Many students or low-income individuals may not have access to credit, savings accounts, or loans from banks. However, through Equb, they can save money collectively and receive a lump-sum amount when their turn arrives. This encourages saving habits, supports emergency needs, and promotes a culture of mutual financial assistance. In this way, Equb plays a crucial role in promoting financial inclusion within the student community.
 
4. Flexibility and Adaptability
Equb is also highly flexible. Members can determine the amount of contribution, the time interval (daily, weekly, or monthly), and the order in which members receive their turns. This flexibility allows groups to customize the system based on their financial capacity and needs. For university students, who often face irregular income or allowance schedules, this adaptability is a key advantage. The existing manual system can easily adjust to the members circumstances without the need for strict formal procedures.
 
5. Strengthening of Social Bonds
Beyond financial benefits, the Equb system strengthens social relationships among participants. Regular meetings and contributions create opportunities for members to interact, share experiences, and offer emotional as well as financial support. This social interaction builds unity and teamwork among university students, helping them develop stronger social networks. Such community support is important for students living away from their families, as it creates a sense of belonging and collective responsibility.
 
6. Low Operational Cost
Another important strength of the existing Equb system is its low operational cost. Since it does not require technological tools, software, or external management, the expenses are minimal. The only requirement is for members to keep a simple record of payments and turns. This makes it a cost-effective method for saving and lending money, especially for small groups of students or community members with limited resources.
 
7. Transparency and Collective Decision-Making
The manual Equb system also promotes transparency and fairness. All members are usually present when contributions and payments are made, which ensures openness in financial transactions. Decisions such as rotation order, penalties for late payments, or membership changes are made collectively. This participatory decision-making process encourages accountability and a sense of ownership among members.
 
8. Motivation for Saving and Financial Discipline
Participating in an Equb encourages members to save regularly. Because contributions are compulsory and consistent, members develop a habit of financial discipline. This is especially beneficial for students who are learning to manage their own finances. The system indirectly teaches budgeting, commitment, and responsibility—skills that are valuable in real-life financial management.
In summary, the existing Equb system, though traditional and manual, has many strengths that contribute to its success and sustainability. Its simplicity, trust-based structure, low cost, and social nature make it an effective financial tool among university students. While it may lack technological efficiency, the human and social factors that define it—trust, cooperation, and community—remain powerful and relevant. These strengths form the foundation upon which an improved, computerized Uni Equb System can be developed to maintain its traditional values while enhancing accessibility, accuracy, and efficiency through modern technology.
2.3 Weakness of Existing System
Although the traditional equb system has been working for quite a long time, there are still several challenges facing this system, especially when it is managed manually among university students. Observing from users and gathering feedback showed the following weaknesses:
1. Manual Operation
The current system does almost everything manually: recording members, collecting contributions, and noting down payments. This makes the process very slow and difficult to manage when the number of participants increases.
2. Common Mistakes
Since it's a manual procedure for calculations and records, small mistakes are easily made that affect the whole contribution or payout process. Errors in dates, amounts, and names are common causes of confusion and complaints.
3. Poor Record Keeping
Most equb groups use paper notebooks and informal listings to record information; such records are easily lost, damaged, or forgotten when the person keeping the record is no longer available.
4. Limited Transparency
In this case, usually only the organizer has access to full details about the contributions and payouts. Members are expected to operate on a basis of trust, which might cause disagreements or suspicions in case something goes wrong.
5. Inconvenient Communication
They mostly notify their members of meetings, dates of collection, or results of draws through social media or by word of mouth. This is very informal and therefore some members may miss such information. 
6. Accessibility Issues
 The current system relies on physical meetings, and that is sometimes a problem for busy members, those living off campus, or especially those who cannot attend regularly. At times, these factors delay contributions, adversely impacting the progress of the group. 
7. Lack of Data Security
 Financial and personal information is stored without any protection. Any person with access to the records can easily view or misuse sensitive data. 
8. No Analytical Insight 
The manual system cannot present any means through which trends can be established, reports generated, or analyses made on how well the group is performing. Every decision is made based on guesswork rather than data. 
 Generally speaking, the current system of equb is time-consuming and subject to human error, which also lacks transparency. This makes it difficult for members to stay updated and have trust in the process. The challenges piling up thus point to the real need for a modern, automated solution such as the UniEqub mobile app, which could make the whole system faster, clearer, and more secure for all the parties,involved.
2.4 Proposed System Description
The system which is called UniEqub tries to solve main challenges happening with normal Equb systems that most university students use. Today, with everything being done by hand, it is difficult for the people. Many problems happen, like losing records, not having enough information visible for everyone and troubles in organizing the group. Because of this, UniEqub is being made to put all these activities into a digital and easier approach. With UniEqub, instead of always managing on paper, there is now a mobile application which helps people handle the Equb in a quicker, clearer and safer way.
With the UniEqub app, students do Equb by creating and checking on their group through phones. This application lets users sign themselves up, make new groups, bring in friends, plus decide the money and how often to pay. Every transfer of cash is put in the system’s storage automatically. This makes human error and confusion not as common as with hand-written records.
Instead of having things put on random pieces of paper or shared using social networks, UniEqub puts all information of the group into one joint database. It is simple for members to go and look at their payment histories, future chances to collect and the way their finances are progressing with the application. This clear look at the Equb builds the trust of members and makes certain that information is not kept away from some people in the group.
For communication in Equb groups, the system also removes several earlier weaknesses. Now, reminders plus notices will come directly from the app, and no person needs to make separate calls or try to remember to inform somebody. Through notifications, people will always remember their payment turn, future events or meetings. It is basically impossible to miss any important thing with automatic alerts.
Security and keeping data safe is an area that the system thinks is very important. Every student requires to sign in using their university ID or an email. Only true students are able to join the groups inside the app. For protection, all transaction and user records will be locked and saved in ways that unauthorized people cannot look at it. Even if the system suddenly fails or removes some information, files can be brought back from special cloud backups.
To sum up, UniEqub finds solutions for almost all big issues that usually trouble the traditional Equb approach by making things more dependable, giving transparency, and being easier to use. It keeps students from needing regular face-to-face meetings, avoids losing important information, and saves time. Also, it encourages the habit of saving and supports teamwork in this organized digital method.
Maybe for later versions, there is a possibility to connect the app directly with local payment tools like Chapa or Telebirr. This would make money transfer very dynamic. It might also connect users from other universities, not just one campus. Some day, even a web version could be made so more people can access it without phones.
So overall, UniEqub is a new solution which mixes technology with the traditional Equb value. By doing this, students use their money better, make financial discipline stronger, and help the campus to have a group that pays more attention to money matters.

CHAPTER THREE: SYSTEM FEATURES
3.1 Introduction
The UNI-Equb system is a digital, mobile-first financial platform that gives college students easy-to-use, organized, and community-focused savings tools.  It uses the traditional "equb" (rotating savings and credit association) model in a new way to help students with their unique financial problems, like having irregular income, sharing expenses, and needing to save for short-term goals.  This chapter talks about the basic features and quality standards that the system needs to meet in order to provide a safe, easy-to-use, and useful service.  The features are designed with a student-centric approach, prioritizing simplicity, trust, and financial inclusion.
3.2 Functional Requirements
Functional requirements describe the core operations and behaviors of the UNI-Equb system.
3.2.1 User Management & Authentication
Registration: The system shall allow new users to create an account by entering their full name, phone number, and password.
Input Validation: The system must validate user inputs for format and uniqueness (specifically checking that the phone number is not already registered).
Authentication: The system shall allow registered users to log in using their phone number and password.
Password Recovery: The system must provide a mechanism for password recovery (e.g., via SMS verification).
Account Lockout: The system shall temporarily lock a user account after multiple failed login attempts.
3.2.2 Equb Cycle Management
Request Cycle Creation: The system shall allow Members to submit a request to create a new Equb group by defining the proposed contribution amount, duration, and rules.
Approve Cycle Submissions: The system shall allow Admins to view pending Equb creation requests and approve or reject them based on platform policies.
Equb Oversight: The system shall allow Admins to suspend or terminate active Equb groups that violate platform rules, without directly intervening in the financial transaction flow.
Browse & Join: The system shall allow members to browse active Equbs and join a group if there are available slots and they meet eligibility criteria.
Real-time Availability: The system must check slot availability in real-time to prevent over-subscription.
Cycle Status: Members shall be able to view the current status of their Equb, including contributions, schedules, and standings.
Cycle Modification: The Treasurer (or Admin) shall be able to update settings, pause, or close Equb cycles, provided the changes do not conflict with active cycles.
3.2.3 Financial Transactions
Contributions: The system must allow members to contribute payments via integrated payment gateways (mobile money, bank transfer, or card).
Payment Tracking: The system shall record successful payments and notify the member immediately.
Payment History: The system shall allow members to view their paginated transaction history, filterable by date range and Equb type.
Payout Withdrawal: The system shall allow the designated winner to request a withdrawal to a linked bank or mobile money account.
Identity Verification: The system must validate the winner's identity and payout details before processing a withdrawal.
3.2.4 Draw & Winner Selection
Random Selection: The system shall execute a random selection algorithm (RNG) to determine the winner of a cycle.
Eligibility Check: The system must verify that all contributions are closed and reconciled before a draw can be initiated.
Notification: The system shall automatically record the winner and notify all participants of the result.
3.2.5 Administration & Reporting
User Oversight: The system shall allow Admins to verify student IDs and manage user account statuses.
Group Audit: The system shall allow Admins to view group activity logs for safety audits.
Compliance Control: The Admin shall have the power to freeze groups flagged for suspicious activity, but cannot manually withdraw or transfer group funds.
Reporting: The system shall generate financial and operational reports (aggregating transactions and membership data) in PDF or CSV formats.
Reminders: The system shall send automated reminders (SMS/Push) to members for upcoming contributions based on the cycle schedule.
3.3 Non-Functional Requirements 
These requirements define the quality attributes, constraints, and performance expectations of the system.
3.3.1 Security & Compliance
Data Integrity: Transaction records must remain immutable after confirmation to ensure financial accuracy.
Secure Payments: The system must integrate with payment gateways using PCI-compliant standards for cards and secure APIs for mobile money.
Auditability (Draws): The system must use a seeded Random Number Generator (RNG) for the lottery process and log the seed and results for audit purposes.
Auditability (Admin Actions): All administrative actions (member changes, rule edits) must be logged in an audit trail.
KYC Compliance: The system must support Know Your Customer (KYC) checks before allowing payout withdrawals.
3.3.2 Performance & Reliability
Network Optimization: The system should be functional on low-bandwidth networks to accommodate users with poor internet connections.
Concurrency: The system must handle concurrent edits and real-time joining requests without data conflicts.
Availability: The system should provide a local cache to allow users to view their status even when offline.
Fault Tolerance: The system must include retry logic and backoff policies for failed notifications or third-party service glitches.
3.3.3 Usability
Error Handling: The system must provide clear, corrective feedback to users when invalid information is entered (e.g., during registration or payment).
Interface: The system must provide specific guidance/empty states when no data is available (e.g., empty payment history).
3.3.4 Scalability & Constraints
Business Rules: An Equb must consist of fixed equal contribution amounts and a defined cycle duration.
Unique Identity: The system shall enforce that a phone number can only be associated with a single user account.

CHAPTER FOUR: SYSTEM DESIGN
4.1 Introduction
This chapter presents the system design of the UNI-Equb application, which is developed to support university students in organizing and managing their savings using a digital Equb system. The system design phase converts the requirements found in previous chapters into a technical and structured blueprint that directs the application's development and implementation.
The design concentrates on how the system will work, how various parts will communicate with one another, and how users will engage with the program. Additionally, it ensures that the system is effective, safe, easy to use, and able to manage students' real-world usage. This chapter lowers development risks and guarantees that the finished system achieves its goals by precisely outlining the system architecture and design components.
4.2 Purpose of the System Design Document
The UNI-Equb application's System Design Document (SDD) aims to give a thorough explanation of the system's construction and functionality. This document provides developers, project managers, and stakeholders with a clear and structured explanation of the system's technical structure.
The system design document specifically seeks to:
To Provide a Road map: It provides the development team with a clear blueprint for the system's construction, demonstrating how various components—such as the database and user interface—work together.
To Ensure Security: Since UNI-Equb handles student money, this document defines the technical rules that keep the data safe and the draws fair.
To Guide Developers: It provides the "how-to" instructions for programmers so they can build the system exactly as it was planned.
 To Save Time and Resources: By planning the system architecture now, we can find and fix potential technical problems before we start writing the code.
4.3 Scope
The scope of this System Design Document (SDD) encompasses the complete architectural and detailed design of the Uni-Equb platform. This design builds directly upon the functional and non-functional requirements defined during the requirement phase.
4.3.1 Design Boundaries
The design scope includes the following core modules:
User Authentication & Security: Detailed design of the authentication system and integration with local KYC (Know Your Customer) standards.
Equb Management: Design of the logic for creating Equb groups, calculating contribution cycles, and managing payout rotations.
Automated Draw System: The technical specification for a fair, verifiable, and automated selection process for winners.
Payment Integration Layer: Architectural design for interfacing with Ethiopian payment gateways (e.g., Telebirr, CBE Birr) to automate transactions.
Notification & Reporting: Design of the background services for SMS/Push reminders and real-time ledger generation for users.
4.4 Architectural Design
The architectural design establishes a structured solution that addresses the technical and operational requirements of UniEqub, with a focus on optimizing performance, security, and maintainability.
Architectural Goals and Constraints
The architecture is primarily shaped by the financial characteristics of the application and the specific needs of the student user base.
 Security: The system ensures complete protection of data. All remote access is subject to user identification (phone number) and password control. Secure communication (HTTPS) is mandatory to protect transaction data.
Availability: The system must be available 24/7 to allow students to check their status or make payments at any time.
Connectivity: The application must function effectively even in low-bandwidth network environments common on campus.
Platform: The client portion resides on Android mobile devices, while the server portion operates on a cloud-hosted environment (Supabase/PostgreSQL).
4.4.1 Logical View of the Architecture
The logical view of the UniEqub system is comprised of three main layers: The User Interface (Presentation) Layer, the Business Services Layer, and the Data Layer.
The logical view of the UniEqub system follows a Layered Architecture pattern, separating concerns into Presentation, Business Logic, and Data Persistence. This separation is critical for financial security, ensuring that the user interface never interacts directly with the database or the core financial ledger.
1. Presentation Layer (Mobile Client)
This layer resides on the user's Android device. It is responsible for input validation and rendering data, but it contains no business rules.
Technology: React Native.
Responsibilities:
Secure Input: Captures user credentials and payment requests.
Real-Time Dashboard: Displays the active state of the Equb (e.g., "Round 3 of 10") by consuming APIs.
Notification Handler: Receives background signals for draw results and payment reminders.
2. Business Services Layer (Application Logic)
This is the core of the system, residing on the cloud server. It processes all requests and enforces the rules of the Equb. It is subdivided into three critical engines:
A. Equb Cycle Manager:
Manages the state of every group (Pending, Active, Locked).
Enforces the "Round" logic, ensuring a new round cannot start until the previous one is reconciled.
Scheduler Service: Triggers the automated draw once all payments for a cycle are verified.
B. Payment Processing Engine (Virtual Account Manager):
This component manages the integration with Chapa.
Virtual Account Logic: To ensure funds are never mixed between groups, the system utilizes a Virtual Account concept. When an Equb group is created, the system generates a unique Payment Reference Key specific to that group.
Transaction Routing: When a member contributes, the payment is tagged with this Reference Key. The Payment Engine listens for Chapa Webhooks to confirm receipt and automatically routes the credit to the specific Group Ledger, ensuring 100% accurate reconciliation without manual intervention.
Isolation: This logic ensures that Group A's funds can never be logically accessed by Group B, mimicking a dedicated bank account for each group.
C. Winner Selection Service:
Contains the Random Number Generator (RNG) logic.
Enforces "Win-Once" rules to ensure a member cannot win twice in the same cycle until the cycle resets.
3. Data Persistence Layer (Database)
This layer manages the storage and retrieval of data while maintaining Referential Integrity.
Technology: PostgreSQL (via Supabase).
Immutable Ledger: The database is designed so that Transaction records are append-only. Once a payment is verified via the Virtual Account logic, it cannot be deleted or altered, providing a secure audit trail for all student funds.
Figure 31:logical view
4.4.3 Process View
The process view describes the tasks and threads involved in the system’s execution. For UniEqub, several key processes run concurrently to ensure smooth operation.
4.4.3.1 Key Processes
1.     User Authentication Process:
o   Manages user login and session security using tokens.
o   Verifies identity against the User table and ensures the account is not locked.
2.     Contribution Tracking Process:
o   Initiated when a member submits a payment.
o   This process communicates with the external Payment Gateway (Chapa) or verifies a manual entry.
o   Upon success, it updates the Transaction table and the Round status.
3.     Equb Cycle & Draw Process:
o   This is a conditional process. It monitors the state of an active round.
o   Condition: IF Total_Contributions == Expected_Total, THEN trigger DrawWinner process.
o   Action: The system runs the random selection algorithm, assigns a WinnerID, and notifies all group members.
4.     Notification Process:
An asynchronous thread that sends SMS or Push Notifications for payment reminders or win announcements.
Figure 32:process view
4.4.4 Payment Architecture: Group-Level Virtual Accounts
To ensure transparent and automated contribution tracking, the system utilizes Chapa Virtual Accounts at the group level.
Group-Specific Accounts: Upon the creation and approval of a new Equb group, the system makes an API call to Chapa to generate a unique Virtual Account Number dedicated solely to that group.
Persistent Validity: This virtual account remains active for the entire duration of the Equb lifecycle (across all rounds). It serves as the digital "pot" for the group.
Automated Reconciliation: When a member transfers their contribution to this Group Virtual Account, Chapa sends a webhook to the UniEqub backend. The system parses the webhook to identify the Sender's Phone Number.The system normalizes the incoming phone number format (converting +251 to 09) and matches this number against the group's member list to automatically mark the specific member as 'Paid' for the current round.
Deactivation: Once the Equb cycle is completed or dissolved, the system marks the Virtual Account as inactive in the database, preventing future deposits.
4.4. Deployment View
The deployment view describes the physical nodes and platform configuration26. The UniEqub system follows a Client-Server deployment model.
Node 1: Android Mobile Device (Client)
Component: UniEqub Mobile Application (.apk).
Function: Provides the interface for students to join groups and view financial history. Requires ~100MB storage and Internet connection.
Node 2: Cloud Server (Backend)
Component: Supabase (hosted PostgreSQL database and Authentication services).
Function: Stores all data, runs the winner selection logic, and manages API requests.
Node 3: External Payment Gateway
Component: Telebirr API / Banking Interface.
Function: Processes real-money transfers and returns transaction status to the UniEqub server.

The primary task of database design in this project is to create logical and physical design models of the centralized database. The design of a logical database concentrates on data requirements, such as the student and payment relationship. The physical database design concentrates on the actual implementation of storing data in a way that is understood and implemented by the database (in this context, PostgreSQL through Supabase), ensuring that data such as financial information and lottery outcomes is independent of physical constraints and other personal identifying information

4.5.2 Relational Mapping & Database Normalization
Relational mapping is the process of converting the conceptual entities identified in the ER model into actual database tables (relations) while establishing the connections between them using Primary Keys (PK) and Foreign Keys (FK). This step bridges the gap between the logical design and the physical implementation in PostgreSQL.
For the UniEqub system, the mapping ensures referential integrity. For instance, a Transaction cannot exist without a valid User and Round. Similarly, the Many-to-Many relationship between Users and Groups is mapped using an intermediate junction table called Group_Members to handle the association efficiently.

4.5.2 Relational Mapping & Database Normalization

Problem Remaining (2NF Limitation)
Even though the Transactions table now satisfies Second Normal Form (2NF), a transitive dependency still exists:
Trans_ID → Round → Winner
The Winner is determined by the Round, not by the individual Transaction. As a result, if all transaction records for a specific round (e.g., Round 1) are deleted, the information about who won that round is also lost. This violates normalization principles and motivates further decomposition in Step 4: Third Normal Form (3NF).
Step 4: Third Normal Form (3NF)
Third Normal Form eliminates transitive dependencies. The Winner attribute is logically determined by the Round, not by individual transactions. Therefore, round-related data is extracted into a separate table.
To resolve this issue, a separate Rounds table was introduced.

CHAPTER FIVE: CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATION
5.1 Conclusion
The reason for initiating UniEqub was to address a limitation of the conventional, manually operated Equb system in a university setting. By making use of contemporary web and mobile technology, UniEqub was able to design an automated finance system that delivers automated operations of group creation, members' registration, and contributions.
This system addresses effectively long-standing challenges associated with the conventional system, such as a lack of transparency, restrictions in terms of geographical location, and human influence in conducting lottery. With a fair lottery system in place through an algorithm, along with statuses in real time, UniEqub inculcates a culture of trust and fiscal responsibility in students and staff.
In addition to that, it shows that it’s possible to develop and re-engineer financial culture practices for modern-day needs and necessities. The application of an efficient technical stack—such as React Native and Supabase—means that not only is it fully functional and operational but it’s also safe and scalable for dealing with sensitive user information. Lastly, UniEqub becomes an essential platform for financial liberation by ensuring students make better use of their finances while enhancing digital inclusion on campus.
5.2 Recommendation 
Real Payment Gateway Integration: Although the current implementation is done in a sandbox, the future versions could be developed with the integration of the real local payment systems such as Telebirr, CBE Birr, or Chapa, enabling live financial operations.
Scalability to Other Institutions: The design of the system is quite flexible and can be applicable on different scales, i.e., not only on one university. In the future versions of Equb, different campuses of universities can also have Equb groups.
Advanced Security Features: The application should also incorporate advanced security mechanisms, including consumer authentication through fingerprint or facial recognition, in addition to security auditing.

Appendix B: Summary of Interview Results (Traditional Equb Collectors)
B.1 Interview Participants
A total of five (5) traditional Equb collectors (organizers) participated in the interview. All participants had practical experience managing one or more Equb groups and were responsible for member coordination, payment collection, and record keeping.
B.2 Summary of Key Interview Findings
The interview data revealed consistent operational patterns and challenges across all participants. The summarized findings are presented below.
Interview Area
Consolidated Findings
Equb groups managed
Collectors manage between 2 and 4 Equb groups simultaneously
Members per group
Groups typically consist of 10–25 members
Contribution amount
Monthly contributions range from 500 to 5,000 ETB
Contribution frequency
All collectors confirmed weekly contributions
Payment punctuality
4 out of 5 collectors reported frequent late payments
Average late members
2–6 members delay payment in each contribution cycle
Payment methods
A hybrid system is used (cash and digital payments)
Payment verification
Mostly manual, relying on SMS or screenshots
Equb turn selection
100% manual (lot drawing or voting)
Turn-related disputes
2 out of 5 collectors experienced disputes
Record keeping
All collectors use paper-based records
Data loss experience
3 out of 5 collectors reported record loss or damage
Technology acceptance
4 out of 5 collectors are willing to use a mobile app
B.3 Key Challenges Identified
The interviews highlighted several critical challenges in traditional Equb management:
Late or missed payments disrupt Equb cycles
Manual payment verification is time-consuming and unreliable
Paper-based record keeping leads to data loss and errors
Lack of transparency in Equb turn selection causes disputes
Managing multiple Equb groups increases administrative burden

The survey results indicate that most students are familiar with Equb practices and show strong interest in a digital alternative. However, successful adoption of the UniEqub mobile application depends on ensuring reliable payments, strong security, and transparent system features.

Qualitative interviews with Equb collectors (Appendix B), and

Late payments

Appendix F: Implications for UniEqub Mobile Application Design
The findings from both data sources informed the design of the UniEqub mobile application, which should:
Support student-friendly Equb group creation
Provide automated payment reminders
Ensure transparent and automated Equb turn selection
Store records digitally and securely
Maintain traditional Equb principles while improving usability
